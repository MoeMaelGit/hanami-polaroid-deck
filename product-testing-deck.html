Hanami Polaroid

Using virtual reality as a product-testing environment: handle a product the way you would in reality, see faithful results, and swap the context or the product in minutes.

XR Class & Design · Carulli A VR product-testing case study
The idea

VR as a product-testing environment

Natural interaction+ Faithful product+ Swappable context & product → evaluate a product before a prototype exists

Hanami Polaroid is a working instance of this, built around a camera: you pick it up, change its settings, see faithful photographs, and you could do it in any scene with any camera.

Why VR for testing

Test before you build

EARLY

Before a prototype

A product can be handled, operated, and judged while it is still a model and a set of parameters.

FAST

Any scenario

Try the same product in many places, lighting conditions, and situations without travelling or rebuilding sets.

FAIR

Side by side

Swap one model for another and compare candidates under identical, repeatable conditions.

Interaction

You handle it like the real thing

  • The camera is picked up by reaching out and grabbing it with your hand, not by pointing a laser and clicking. Far-pointer grabbing is rejected.
  • Holding, raising, and aiming the camera mirror the real gesture, so what a tester learns about handling the product carries over to the real one.
Fidelity

The product behaves like the product

  • Not a prop with a fake screen: a physically simulated instrument. Zoom, focus, aperture, and depth of field are computed as a real lens.
  • They genuinely shape the image: open the aperture and the background softens; rack focus and the subject sharpens; zoom and the depth of field change together, from one consistent lens.
A tester can answer "what would a photo from this camera, at these settings, actually look like?" without owning the camera.
Output

The result is a real object

  • A captured photo ejects as a physical polaroid that falls, can be picked up, shaken to develop, and hung on a line.
  • It obeys gravity and collides with the world like the real thing, closing the loop between operating the product and judging its result.
Configurable · 1 of 2

Swap the environment

Garden→ City street· Interior· Studio· Low light· Bright exterior

Changing the world changes the lighting, the subjects, and the feel, so the same camera can be evaluated across many situations and locations quickly, instead of building a new test for each.

Configurable · 2 of 2

A camera library

  • The camera's behaviour is model-agnostic and bound to the model by its components, so a different camera can be dropped in and operated by the same mechanisms.
  • That makes a library of cameras possible: several models, tested and compared under identical conditions.
The lens characteristics, focal range, aperture, sensor, are values, not hard-coded behaviour, so a specific real product can be reproduced from its specs and evaluated.
Evaluate

What you can measure

HANDLINGDoes the product feel natural to hold and operate? Are the controls reachable and intuitive?
OUTPUT QUALITYWhat does the product produce across its settings (here, how photographs look at different zoom, focus, aperture)?
VARIANT COMPARISONTwo or more models, same user, identical conditions, compare preference and performance.
SCENARIO FITHow the same product performs across different environments, lighting, and situations.
USER BEHAVIOURHow people approach, hold, and use the product when it behaves realistically.
Beyond the camera

It generalises, and where it goes next

  • The pattern, natural interaction + faithful product + swappable context, applies to many handheld or operated products.
  • Modular and lightweight: interaction, product, and environment are separate, interchangeable pieces. A new test is an extension, not a rebuild.

TOWARD A TESTING PLATFORM

  • Measurement & capture of actions, settings, outputs, timing.
  • A product & environment catalogue combined on demand.
  • Validation against real-world testing.
  • Multi-user, guided sessions.
