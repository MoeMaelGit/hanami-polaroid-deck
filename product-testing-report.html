A case study in using virtual reality as a product-testing environment: evaluating a product through natural, realistic interaction in contexts and configurations that can be swapped in minutes.

01 Overview

Hanami Polaroid began as an interactive VR experience: the user walks a Japanese garden, picks up a digital camera, frames shots through a live viewfinder, and takes pictures that eject as physical polaroids to be shaken and hung.

Read a second way, it is a working demonstration of something more general: a virtual-reality environment can serve as a testbed for products. The "product" here is a camera, and in VR you handle it the way you would in reality, change its settings, and see faithful results, all inside a scene you can completely replace. This report sets out why that matters for product testing and which parts of the project already demonstrate it.

The core idea is simple. If you can interact with a product naturally, simulate it faithfully, and swap both the context and the product at will, you have a place to evaluate how it feels and performs before any physical prototype exists.

02 VR as a product-testing environment

Testing a physical product traditionally needs physical prototypes, real test locations, and time. Each variant, and each scenario you want to try it in, costs more of all three. A virtual environment changes that economics:

  • Test before you build. A product can be handled, operated, and judged while it is still a 3D model and a set of parameters.
  • Change the scenario instantly. The same product can be tried in many places, lighting conditions, and situations without travelling or rebuilding sets.
  • Compare variants side by side. Swapping one model for another is a configuration change, so testers can compare candidates under identical conditions.
  • Safe, repeatable, observable. Every session runs under the same controlled conditions and can be repeated exactly.

Hanami Polaroid is a concrete, working instance of this idea, built around a camera.

03 Natural, realistic interaction

For a VR test to tell you anything true about a product, the interaction has to feel like the real thing. This is where the project is strongest.

You handle the product the way you would in reality

The camera is picked up by reaching out and grabbing it with your hand, not by pointing a laser and clicking. Far-pointer grabbing is explicitly rejected. The result is that holding, raising, and aiming the camera mirror the real gesture, so what a tester learns about handling it carries over.

The product behaves like the real product

The camera is not a prop with a fake screen. It is a physically simulated instrument: zoom, focus, aperture, and depth of field are computed as a real lens, and they genuinely shape the image you capture. Open the aperture and the background softens; rack focus and the subject sharpens; zoom in and the framing and the depth of field change together, because the optics are driven by one consistent lens model rather than separate cosmetic effects.

Because the settings produce a faithful result, a tester can answer "what would a photograph from this camera, at these settings, actually look like?" without owning the camera.

The output is a real, handleable object

A captured photo ejects as a physical polaroid that falls, can be picked up, shaken to develop, and hung on a line. It obeys gravity and collides with the world like the real thing. The artefact of the product, the photograph, is itself something the tester holds and inspects, closing the loop between operating the product and judging its result.

It is comfortable to use

Comfort was treated as a requirement, not a finishing touch: there is no nausea-inducing motion, the framed-image effects never touch the player's own eyes, and turning and movement are tuned to stay easy. A tester can concentrate on the product instead of fighting the medium, which is what makes the feedback trustworthy.

04 Configurable test scenarios

The reason this generalises into a testing tool is that almost everything around the product is interchangeable.

Swap the environment

The scene is built so the setting can be replaced. The garden is one context; the same product could be tested in a city street, an interior, a studio, a dim room, or a bright exterior. Changing the world changes the lighting, the subjects, and the feel, so the same camera can be evaluated across many situations and locations quickly, rather than building a new test for each.

Swap the product: a camera library

The camera's behaviour is driven by general, model-agnostic logic and bound to the model by its components, so a different camera can be dropped in and operated by the same mechanisms. That makes a library of cameras possible: several models, each tested and compared under identical conditions, to see how each would behave and what kind of images each would produce.

Parameterise the product

The lens characteristics, focal range, aperture range, and sensor, are values rather than hard-coded behaviour, so a specific real product can be reproduced by entering its specifications and then evaluated in VR. The same applies to how the photographs come out, which follows directly from those parameters.

05 What can be evaluated

HANDLINGDoes the product feel natural to hold and operate? Are the controls reachable and intuitive in the user's hands?
OUTPUT QUALITYWhat does the product actually produce across its settings (here, how photographs look at different zoom, focus, and aperture)?
VARIANT COMPARISONPut two or more models in front of the same user under identical conditions and compare preference and performance.
SCENARIO FITHow does the same product perform across different environments, lighting, and use situations?
USER BEHAVIOURHow do people approach, hold, and use the product when it behaves realistically?

06 Why it generalises beyond a camera

Nothing about the approach is specific to cameras. The pattern, natural hand interaction, a faithfully simulated product, and swappable context and product, applies to many handheld or operated products where how something feels to use and what it produces both matter.

This is practical because the project is built in a modular, lightweight way: the interaction behaviours, the product, and the environment are separate, interchangeable pieces rather than one fixed scene. Adding a new product, a new environment, or a new interaction is an extension, not a rebuild, which is what keeps the cost of standing up a new test low.

07 Toward a product-testing platform

As a research direction, a small number of additions would turn this case study into a general testing tool:

  • Measurement and capture. Record what the tester does, the settings they choose, the images they produce, and timing, so sessions yield data, not just impressions.
  • A product and environment catalogue. Curated libraries of products and contexts that can be combined on demand for a given test.
  • Validation against reality. Compare VR-test outcomes with real-world testing of the same products to calibrate how well the virtual results predict the physical ones.
  • Multi-user and guided sessions. Structured tasks and the ability to run many testers through the same protocol.

08 Conclusion

Hanami Polaroid shows, in a working form, the ingredients a VR product-testing environment needs: a product you handle naturally, that behaves and produces results faithfully, set inside a context and a product slot you can swap at will. Used this way, virtual reality offers a fast, low-cost, repeatable place to evaluate how a product feels and performs, across many scenarios and many variants, well before a physical prototype is made.
